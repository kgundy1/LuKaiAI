## What LuKaiAI is at its core

LuKaiAI is a free workflow teaching non-technical people to build software with Claude.

The name comes from Kevin's two sons: **Lu**cas and **Kai**ler. The "AI" superscript is the technology layer. The wordmark colors (lu-gold, kai-white, ai-cyan) are sacred — never substituted.

The product currently lives at **lukaiai.com**. The course consists of 7 modules and 35 lessons. As of session 4 (May 29, 2026), the platform has 6 registered learners and is running on Supabase (migrated from Render Postgres).

The methodology being taught: the Claude Chat / Claude Code split. Chat for planning and capture-and-ask reasoning; Code for execution. The whole platform was built using this methodology — LuKaiAI IS the proof of method.

## The audience framing rule (load-bearing)

**The single most important rule in the entire system:** treat the reader as someone with an **idea they want to build**, not as someone with a **problem to solve**.

- ✅ Sell toward a **possibility** (ambition)
- ❌ Sell against a **problem** (pain)

**On-voice:**
- "You've got an idea. Let's build it."
- "Whatever you want to make — a tool for your team, an app for your hobby, something for your kids — you can build it."
- "Anyone with an idea deserves to bring it to life."

**Off-voice (never use):**
- "Tired of expensive developers?"
- "Struggling to bring your ideas to life?"
- "Frustrated by the high cost of software?"

This rule isn't aesthetic. It's strategic. LuKaiAI's audience is people with ambition who feel locked out of building. The promise is "you can do this, here's how." NOT "look how bad your current situation is."

The rest of the voice — quiet confidence, no condescension, short declarative sentences — hangs off this rule.

## The affordability promise

**The course stays free for individuals. Forever. This is non-negotiable.**

The promise as stated: "The right to build shouldn't cost thousands."

Specific phrasings that work:
- "Free. The full course. No upsells."
- "Yours to keep. No subscription."

Specific framings that DON'T work (look like spam, undercut quiet confidence):
- "100% FREE!!"
- Comparisons that name competitor prices

This is part of the brand identity, not a marketing tactic. Future-Kevin: if you ever consider charging individuals for course access, the answer is no. There are other revenue paths (see below). The free course is what makes LuKaiAI different from every other "learn to code with AI" offering.

## The strategic vision — three-phase revenue evolution

This is what makes LuKaiAI a long-arc business, not just a free course. The current state is Phase 1. Each subsequent phase requires the previous one to be working.

### Phase 1 — Free course as the funnel (current state, May 2026)

- Free course at lukaiai.com
- Captures email signups
- Builds learner base
- Generates real shipped projects (the /projects showcase shipped in Session 3)
- Success metric: number of learners who ship something real

The course itself doesn't make money. It exists to:
1. Prove the methodology works at scale (not just for Kevin)
2. Build a body of evidence (shipped projects) that becomes the credibility foundation for Phase 2 and 3
3. Build distribution (learners who tell other people)
4. Establish LuKaiAI as a known entity in the "AI-assisted building" space

**Triggers to move toward Phase 2:** 50+ shipped projects in the showcase, repeated organic outreach asking "can you build this for me?", or a partnership opportunity that's too good to ignore.

### Phase 2 — Partnerships and B2B (future)

When the free course has built enough proof and reach, LuKaiAI becomes a platform that other entities want to partner with. Possible shapes:

- **Anthropic partnership.** LuKaiAI demonstrates AI-assisted building at the individual level. Could become a referenced training pathway, co-marketing partner, or pre-built curriculum for Claude.
- **Enterprise training.** Companies want their non-engineering staff to be able to build internal tools. LuKaiAI becomes the canonical curriculum for that, sold B2B.
- **Tool partnerships.** Cursor, Replit, Vercel, etc. — companies whose tools LuKaiAI teaches. Affiliate or co-marketing partnerships.

The course stays free for individuals throughout Phase 2. Revenue comes from organizations, not learners.

**Triggers to move toward Phase 3:** Phase 2 generates enough revenue to be Kevin's primary income.

### Phase 3 — Brand asset (longer-term future)

When LuKaiAI is broadly known and the body of shipped projects has compounded, the brand itself becomes valuable. Possible paths:

- Acquisition by a larger education or AI company
- Standalone media business (LuKaiAI-branded content, books, conference)
- Foundation/community (LuKaiAI as a movement, with staff and operations)

This phase is speculative. The shape it takes depends entirely on what Phase 1 and 2 actually become.

## The productized service idea (sidecar, not core)

Captured in a previous session and worth preserving:

**Concept:** A productized service where clients fill out a structured questionnaire (~20 minutes, 15-25 questions) describing what they want built. Kevin builds it using the LuKaiAI methodology. Tiered fixed pricing based on what the form reveals.

**Pricing tiers (rough):**
- ~$5-10k — static prototype, no backend
- ~$15-30k — full-stack with database
- ~$30-60k — multi-user with payments/integrations

**Why this could work:**
- The methodology is proven (LuKaiAI itself is the proof)
- Kevin speaks both languages (non-technical client → technical implementation)
- Build speed is real (Module 6 went from audit to live in ~90 min during course development)

**Open questions to resolve before launching:**
- Change-order policy (clients always add scope)
- Maintenance/handoff model (do you keep them as ongoing clients, or hand off?)
- Lead generation/distribution (where do clients come from?)

**Relationship to core LuKaiAI:** This is a sidecar revenue stream that could fund Phase 1 while the strategic plan plays out. NOT the core business model. The free course remains the strategic asset.

**Decision deferred:** Don't start this until either (a) the free course is running steadily without needing daily attention, or (b) someone proactively asks Kevin to build for them.

## The Facebook pattern (reference)

Reference model from yesterday's strategic conversation, worth preserving:

Facebook started as a free product. The product itself didn't make money. But the user base, network effects, and the data those users generated became the asset that enabled multiple later revenue paths (advertising, marketplace, payment processing, etc.).

LuKaiAI's free course is the same structural pattern. The course itself doesn't make money. But the learner base, the shipped-projects showcase, and the credibility that compounds over time become the asset that enables Phase 2 and 3.

**The strategic test:** Does today's decision build the asset (course quality, learner experience, shipped-projects body of evidence)? If yes, do it. If no, don't.

The /projects showcase shipped in Session 3 is a literal application of this thinking — it's an asset that compounds, NOT a feature that converts.

## Guardrails — things that stay true across all phases

These are constraints LuKaiAI commits to, regardless of how the business evolves:

1. **The course stays free for individuals. Forever.** Phase 2 and 3 revenue comes from organizations, never from blocking individual access.
2. **The audience framing rule is permanent.** Ambition, not pain. No matter what marketing pressure surfaces.
3. **The proof-of-concept reference rule stays.** Generic dealership-group framing, never the specific employer details.
4. **The voice stays quiet.** No hype, no exclamation marks, no "transform your life" copy. Even when the business pressure says louder = better.
5. **Lucas and Kailer come first.** LuKaiAI exists in service of Kevin's life and family, not the other way around. If the work demands sacrificing real family time, the work scales back. Not the family.

---

## Anti-goals — things LuKaiAI explicitly is NOT

Equally important to capture:

1. **Not a bootcamp.** LuKaiAI doesn't promise to make anyone a software engineer. It teaches a workflow for building specific software with AI assistance.
2. **Not a credentialing program.** No certificates, no badges, no "LuKaiAI Certified" titles. The proof is what learners ship, not what LuKaiAI signs.
3. **Not a community platform.** LuKaiAI may have a community over time, but it's not the product. The product is the methodology + the workflow.
4. **Not aggressive growth.** No paid acquisition, no growth-hacking, no manipulative onboarding. Distribution comes from real shipped projects and word-of-mouth.
5. **Not a SaaS.** Even when Phase 2 or 3 brings revenue, LuKaiAI doesn't become a software product Kevin maintains forever. It stays a course, a workflow, a methodology — things with relatively low ongoing maintenance burden.

## The financial reality framing

Captured from earlier strategic conversation. Important for grounding any business decisions:

**Kevin is the sole household income source.** This affects every strategic decision:

- LuKaiAI cannot be Kevin's primary income for the foreseeable future (Phase 1 generates $0).
- The sustainable work pace for LuKaiAI is "many hours per week," not "full-time."
- Any revenue path that requires Kevin to work full-time on LuKaiAI before Phase 2 is generating real money is off the table.
- The productized service (sidecar) could potentially generate enough side income to reduce dependence on day job, but only if it doesn't consume so much time that LuKaiAI itself stalls.

**What "success" looks like in concrete terms:**

- **Year 1 (now-ish):** Course is good, learners ship things, /projects showcase has 20+ entries, LuKaiAI is a known name in a small but real niche.
- **Year 2:** Phase 2 revenue starts. Partnerships or B2B training pays meaningfully. Kevin reduces day-job dependence.
- **Year 3+:** LuKaiAI is sustainable as Kevin's primary work, OR a clean acquisition / brand-asset path materializes.

Failure modes to recognize:
- Burning out before year 2 because the pace was unsustainable
- Pivoting to a paid model and destroying the brand identity
- Building the platform AT THE EXPENSE of the course content itself
